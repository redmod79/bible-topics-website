OBSERVATIONS:
1. ALL five vow constructions follow the SAME grammatical template (im +
   inf.abs.+impf. protasis / weqatal apodosis).
2. In four out of five (Jacob, Hannah, Absalom, Israel/Num 21), the default
   conjunctive-weqatal force is uncontroversial. The apodosis expresses one
   reward or one action (or a coordinated sequence).
3. Num 21:2 uses חרם (cherem) not נדר (neder)-plus-olah. That vow's apodosis
   (wehacharamti) is a PUBLIC JUDICIAL BAN, lawful in conquest against a
   Canaanite oppressor (Arad). It is NOT a private person-vow like Jephthah's.
4. Hannah (1 Sam 1:11) is the lexically closest dedication-model: SAME
   "laYHWH" dative frame, PLUS explicit "all the days of his life" +
   separation-marker. Jephthah omits both.
5. The disjunctive-waw reading of Jdg 11:31, if genuinely admissible, would
   be a UNIQUE case — no other OT vow demonstrably uses disjunctive waw in
   the apodosis chain.

NOTE ON LXX TEXT COMPARISON (see grammar-reference/greek/lxx-disjunctive-waw-vows.md)
================================================================

LXX Jdg 11:31:
  A-text: καὶ ἔσται τῷ κυρίῳ καὶ ἀνοίσω αὐτὸν ὁλοκαύτωμα
          ("and it shall be to the LORD and I will offer him up [as]
          whole-burnt-offering")
          → contested waw rendered with KAI (conjunctive)
  B-text: καὶ ἔσται τῷ κυρίῳ· ἀνοίσω αὐτὸν ὁλοκαύτωμα
          ("and it shall be to the LORD; I will offer him up...")
          → asyndeton (no conjunction)

Neither recension uses ἤ ("or") or εἰ μή ("unless"). Both Greek particles
were available to the translators (used elsewhere for genuine disjunction,
e.g., Lev 5:1 ἤ...ἤ for אוֹ... אוֹ). Their ABSENCE at Jdg 11:31 is substantive
negative evidence against the disjunctive-waw reading. The earliest pre-
Christian Jewish readers did NOT see disjunction here.

LXX Jdg 5:11: διηγέομαι (G1334) "recount"
LXX Jdg 11:40: θρηνεῖν (G2354) "lament"

The LXX translators split the dis legomenon תנה into two Greek verbs, one
celebratory and one mournful. The LXX Jdg 11:40 choice of θρηνεῖν reflects
the sacrificial-death reading of the narrative. But LXX Jdg 5:11 confirms
that the Hebrew root itself in Piel carries "recount/celebrate" force.
